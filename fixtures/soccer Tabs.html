2026 FIFA World Cup

Three host nations. Three football stories.

Canada, Mexico and the United States share the 2026 World Cup. Each country brings a different football history to the tournament.

Canada

A fast-growing football nation

Canada’s men’s team reached the World Cup in 1986, returned in 2022, and now enters 2026 as a host nation.

  • World Cup marker Canada’s first men’s World Cup appearance was in Mexico in 1986.
  • Recent shift Canada returned to the men’s World Cup in 2022 after a 36-year wait.
  • 2026 meaning Canada is hosting men’s World Cup matches for the first time.

Key takeaway

Canada’s story is about momentum. The 2026 tournament is a chance to turn recent progress into a bigger football culture.

Mexico

A World Cup regular with deep roots

Mexico has one of the richest World Cup histories in North America. It played in the first tournament in 1930 and hosted the event in 1970 and 1986.

  • World Cup marker Mexico becomes the first country to host or co-host the men’s World Cup three times.
  • Best finish Mexico reached the quarter-finals in 1970 and 1986. Both runs came on home soil.
  • 2026 meaning Mexico brings memory, pressure and tradition. Tiny assignment. No big deal.

Key takeaway

Mexico’s story is about legacy. Its supporters expect the national team to match the weight of its football history.

United States

A football market still writing its story

The United States played in the first World Cup in 1930. Its best finish came that year, when it reached the semi-finals.

  • World Cup marker The United States hosted the men’s World Cup in 1994, a major moment for the sport’s growth there.
  • Long-term impact The 1994 tournament helped build momentum for professional soccer in the United States.
  • 2026 meaning The United States hosts again with a much larger soccer audience than it had in 1994.

Key takeaway

The United States story is about scale. The question is no longer whether soccer can grow. It is how far it can go.
